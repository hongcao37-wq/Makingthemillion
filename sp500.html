What Is the S&P 500?

The S&P 500 is a stock market index that tracks the performance of 500 of the largest publicly traded companies in the United States. It includes giants like Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Nvidia, Google, Tesla, and hundreds more across every major sector.

Created in 1957 by Standard & Poor’s, it’s widely regarded as the best gauge of the overall U.S. stock market and the economy.

When people say “the market is up,” they’re almost always talking about the S&P 500.

Why the S&P 500 Beats Most Alternatives

  • Diversification built-in: 500 companies across all sectors reduces risk.
  • Low cost: Index funds tracking it have expense ratios as low as 0.03%.
  • Outperforms most active managers: Over 90% of professional fund managers fail to beat the S&P 500 over 15+ years.
  • Survives everything: It has recovered from every crash in history and gone on to new highs.
  • Simple and hands-off: Perfect for busy people who want strong returns without stock-picking stress.
"Far more money has been made by people investing in an S&P 500 index fund than by all the active stock pickers combined." — Warren Buffett

How to Invest in the S&P 500

You don’t buy the index directly — you invest through low-cost index funds or ETFs that track it.

Top recommended options (as of 2026):

  • Vanguard S&P 500 ETF (VOO) — Expense ratio: 0.03%
  • iShares Core S&P 500 ETF (IVV) — Expense ratio: 0.03%
  • SPDR S&P 500 ETF Trust (SPY) — The most traded, expense ratio: 0.09%
  • Vanguard 500 Index Fund (VFIAX) — Mutual fund version

Steps to get started:

  1. Open an account at a brokerage (Vanguard, Fidelity, Charles Schwab — all excellent and low-cost).
  2. Deposit money you won’t need for 5+ years.
  3. Buy shares of VOO, IVV, or SPY.
  4. Set up automatic monthly investments.
  5. Hold for the long term — ignore short-term noise.

Final Thought: Keep It Simple

You don’t need to be a genius or spend hours researching stocks to build serious wealth. The S&P 500 has made millionaires out of ordinary teachers, nurses, and factory workers who simply invested consistently over decades.

The million isn’t found in hot tips or timing the market. It’s found in owning a piece of America’s most successful companies — and giving them time to grow.
